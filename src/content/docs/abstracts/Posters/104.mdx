Medicinal and oilseed plants are valuable sources of bioactive compounds with antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and pharmaceutical properties. However, drought stress is one of the major environmental factors limiting plant growth, productivity, and metabolic homeostasis. Understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying drought tolerance is therefore essential for the development of stress-resilient crops and sustainable agriculture.

In the present study, label-free quantitative proteomics combined with bioinformatic analyses was employed to investigate drought-responsive proteome changes in contrasting plant genotypes, including a drought-tolerant mutant and its wild-type counterpart. Plants were grown under control and drought stress conditions, and total proteins were extracted from leaf tissues using an extraction buffer supplemented with protease inhibitors. Protein concentrations were quantified prior to enzymatic digestion using trypsin. The resulting peptides were analyzed using liquid chromatography coupled with tandem mass spectrometry (LC–MS/MS).

Raw mass spectrometry data were processed using MaxQuant software for protein identification and label-free quantification against a reference protein database. Downstream statistical and bioinformatic analyses were performed in Perseus software, including normalization, principal component analysis (PCA), hierarchical clustering, volcano plot analysis, and differential abundance testing.

More than 7,000 proteins were identified across all experimental samples. Comparative proteomic analyses revealed distinct drought-responsive protein expression patterns between mutant and wild-type plants. Several candidate proteins potentially involved in oxidative stress regulation, stress signaling pathways, and adaptive responses to water deficit were identified. In particular, proteins associated with redox homeostasis and stress-responsive molecular networks exhibited differential abundance under drought conditions.

Overall, this study highlights the potential of label-free quantitative proteomics and bioinformatic approaches for uncovering molecular signatures associated with abiotic stress tolerance in plants.
