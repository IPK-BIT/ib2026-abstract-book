Deciphering the intricate mapping between linear biomolecular sequences and their complex biological functions remains a central and enduring challenge in the fields of genomics and synthetic biology. In recent years, the rapid evolution of foundation models for nucleic acid sequence modeling has demonstrated that biological sequences can be effectively treated as a learnable language. However, a significant limitation persists: existing generative nucleotide language models generally lack explicit access to high-level biological semantics. Most current generative models rely primarily on sequence prefixes, local context, or limited structured attributes for conditional modeling, which struggle to capture complex, context-dependent biological functions. Natural language, in contrast, provides a vastly more flexible representation that is highly suitable for expressing human intent in sequence design.

To address this critical limitation, we present GenNA, a generative nucleotide foundation model explicitly guided by natural-language annotations. GenNA is built upon a decoder-only Transformer architecture, configured with reference to the Qwen3 design paradigm, and comprises approximately 3.6 billion parameters. Central to its design is an autoregressive framework that incorporates natural-language prompts—such as species identity, gene names, and functional annotations—directly at the beginning of the sequence to guide generation. The model was pretrained from scratch on a massive multimodal nucleotide-text corpus spanning 2,221 eukaryotic species and comprising roughly 416 billion characters, derived from the NCBI RefSeq database. To accommodate the heterogeneous nature of this data, we trained a unified cross-modal byte-pair encoding (BPE) tokenizer with a vocabulary of 6,000 tokens. This approach maps continuous nucleotide sequences, natural-language functional descriptions, and XML-style structural tags into a shared discrete token space, enabling joint modeling under a causal language modeling objective.

Our systematic evaluations demonstrate that GenNA successfully internalizes the complex relationships between sequence syntax and biological semantics. During sequence-function consistency testing, systematically substituting natural-language functional annotations to create mismatches with the underlying nucleotide sequences resulted in substantially elevated perplexity (PPL) scores. Furthermore, token-level attention analysis during coding sequence (CDS) generation revealed that prompt tokens consistently accounted for an average of 64.4% of the model's attention weight. This demonstrates that semantic functional prompts are not merely superficial auxiliary information, but actively participate in the continuous probabilistic evaluation and generation process.

Furthermore, GenNA exhibits a profound zero-shot capacity to predict mutation effects and capture evolutionary constraints. Through in silico mutational scanning on protein-coding genes, we found that single-nucleotide mutations in non-coding regions and synonymous substitutions caused only modest changes in perplexity. In sharp contrast, mutations altering the protein product triggered strong rejection signals: missense mutations yielded a median ΔPPL approximately four-fold higher than synonymous mutations, while frameshift mutations produced the most drastic perturbative effects (median ΔPPL = 5.411). This indicates the model implicitly discriminates coding constraints and local abnormalities without any task-specific fine-tuning on variant data. GenNA also successfully maps phylogenetic representations. Visualizing intermediate-layer representations revealed distinct clustering patterns corresponding to broad biological taxa, forming relatively well-defined subspaces for different species groups. Swapping species labels in the input prompts predictably increased perplexity in alignment with the degree of evolutionary divergence, yielding a ΔPPL matrix that topologically mirrored established biological phylogenetic relationships.

Crucially, GenNA excels in controllable, natural-language-guided nucleotide sequence generation spanning open-ended and highly constrained tasks. In minimal unconditional generation scenarios, the model autoregressively generated complete metadata and sequences that proved highly semantically consistent when validated against existing databases. In species-specific generation across diverse model organisms, GenNA successfully reproduced species-associated genomic footprints, including distinct GC-content distributions, GC3 variations, and codon-usage biases.

For targeted generation of structured non-coding RNAs, we prompted GenNA to design tRNAs across 61 distinct anticodons. The generated candidates achieved high Cove scores in systematic tRNAscan-SE evaluations, respecting structural constraints. Notably, the model spontaneously adopted natural anticodon-recognition strategies, including wobble pairing, and accurately modeled sequence length variations characteristic of Type II tRNAs. Similarly, generated ribosomal RNAs (5S, 5.8S, 18S, and 28S) yielded bit-score distributions overall close to those of real rRNA reference sets when aligned to Rfam covariance models. For protein-coding sequences, we challenged the model to generate sequences for five major histone classes (H1, H2A, H2B, H3, H4). GenNA successfully recapitulated the principal biophysical profiles of these families within molecular-weight (MW) and isoelectric-point (pI) space. We also observed that tuning autoregressive sampling parameters provided substantial control at inference time, allowing smooth transitions between conservative targeted generation and open-ended sequence exploration.

In conclusion, GenNA fills a critical gap between sequence modeling and semantics-driven design in current nucleotide foundation models. By explicitly incorporating natural language, species context, and functional metadata into an autoregressive framework, it provides a unified approach for nucleotide sequence understanding and conditional generation. While future work will address necessary wet-lab validations and sampling calibration to further optimize sequence diversity, GenNA establishes a promising interface for transforming high-level biological intentions into robust sequence design.
